Introduction

Hi there

I am currently pursuing a Master degree at the Institute of Medicine, Chung Shan Medical University, Taiwan. My research interests include wearable devices, child psychiatry, Electrocardiogram (ECG), Electromyography (EMG),electroencephalogram (EEG), arrhythmia, medical engineering, human signals, and machine learning.

I'm fascinated by signals. Recently, I've been using CoP (Center of Pressure) and combining it with IMFs/EMD to decompose human body sway amplitude signals. I hope to make a breakthrough when integrating this with EMG. I'm also particularly fond of EKG. During my clinical internship, I learned a lot about the changes in the heart within just a few seconds. The instantaneous appearance of signals is truly captivating to me! It's like something that can be encountered but not sought after.

As you can see, my college GPA was very low early (you can find on "Hospital life" this pages that why, and I made progress!). My health began to improve in the spring of 2022. Despite undergoing treatment in college, I was determined not to give up. I truly started my research journey in June 2023. You might be wondering, "How did you manage to achieve such substantial research output in just two years?" To be honest, I don't have a biological family, which allows me to focus more on myself. I also prefer spending time in nature rather than using social media. In my spare time, I enjoy drawing with classmates from different countries and discussing our cultures, which is incredibly enriching. Occasionally, we even cook each other’s hometown dishes together.

    When I grow up

    My passion for research was profoundly shaped by personal experiences. Growing up in a traditional family marred by domestic violence, I was ultimately abandoned and denied educational opportunities due to my gender. From the age of 17, I spent seven years under the care of a Hospital, undergoing various treatments and surgeries. This challenging period initially impacted my academic performance, resulting in a low bachelor's GPA (2.9/4.0) in the early stages. However, I persevered, recognizing that "ignorance" is truly the most formidable adversary. My dedication led to significant improvement in my later academic performance (B.S last 60 credits: 3.13/4.0, M.S GPA: 4.0/4.0).

    The medical professionals who cared for me played a pivotal role in my recovery and development. Not only did they help restore my health, but they also continually encouraged my academic growth and imparted valuable medical knowledge. Due to my medical condition, I was constantly connected to various medical devices. This unique circumstance led me to question how physiological signals, such as heart rate and brain waves, could indicate conditions like panic attacks. My daily experiences with medical equipment sparked a deep curiosity about human signals, laying the foundation for my current research interests.

    Despite my primarily medical background, I was fortunate to have a friend studying signals, which provided me with invaluable insights into engineering.

    Neurotransmitters and Stress

    Cortisol is a crucial hormone that helps the human body manage stress, with its secretion typically increasing during stressful situations. Similarly, adrenaline surges during acute stress, leading to elevated heart rate and blood pressure—key elements of the fight-or-flight response. On the other hand, dopamine is often referred to as the "feel-good" hormone, as it promotes feelings of happiness. Interestingly, dopamine levels can also rise under stress. Numerous studies have shown that when the levels of cortisol, adrenaline, and dopamine are abnormally low, it may contribute to the development of depression.

    Brain wave

    Brain wave is divided into five major types, each of which corresponds to different brain states and activities.

    EEG

    The left and right sides of the frontal lobe are labeled as F3 and F4, respectively. This is because the frontal lobe plays a crucial role in brain activities related to emotions, thinking, and behavior.

    Other electrodes also have corresponding functions. For example, O1 and O2 on the occipital lobe are primarily related to visual processing.

    Polysomnography (PSG)

    A good tool named Polysomnography (PSG) is a comprehensive sleep study used to diagnose sleep disorders. It records various physiological parameters during sleep, allowing healthcare providers to assess sleep quality and identify issues. A standard PSG is composed of EEG, EOG, EMG, EKG, oxygen levels, heart rate, and breathing to assess sleep stages. But it doesn't look like a very pleasant experience, you have to sleep all night in a place you are unfamiliar…Anyway.

    REM and Deep Sleeping

    If we use the familiar computer as an analogy, REM is the function of temporarily storing information and refreshing it, and NREM is for storing long-term memory consolidation. Both are essential for regulating emotions. Disruption in either phase can lead to issues such as fatigue, memory problems, emotional instability, and reduced cognitive function.

    Sleeping Cycle

    We can see that sleep is cyclical, starting with N1 where muscles begin to relax and heart rate slows down. In N3, the brain enters its deepest state of rest before gradually returning to REM. Typically, one cycle lasts 90 minutes.

    Childrenhood trauma research

    Violence affects brain regions

    Early childhood violence can have profound effects on brain development, particularly in regions associated with emotion regulation, stress response, and cognitive function. Exposure to violence during formative years can lead to alterations in the structure and function of the brain.

    1. Amygdala

    This region is crucial for processing emotions, especially fear. Children exposed to violence may experience hyperactivity in the amygdala, leading to heightened anxiety and emotional dysregulation.

    2. Prefrontal Cortex:

    Responsible for executive functions such as decision-making, impulse control, and social behavior, the prefrontal cortex can be negatively impacted by early trauma. This may result in difficulties with attention, planning, and managing emotions.

    3. Hippocampus:

    This area is essential for memory formation and learning. Chronic exposure to violence can lead to reduced hippocampal volume, impairing memory and the ability to learn new information.

    Low self-esteem and guilt caused by trauma.

    Nightmare

    Nightmares can serve as a manifestation of underlying trauma, impacting brain development and emotional health. Addressing the root causes of nightmares is essential for promoting healthier sleep patterns and overall well-being.

    Dissociative amnesia

    Psychological Defense

    Dissociative amnesia is a psychological condition characterized by an inability to recall important personal information, usually related to a traumatic or stressful event, that cannot be explained by ordinary forgetfulness.

    This memory loss is often selective, affecting specific events or periods of time, and is not due to substance use, neurological conditions, or other medical disorders.
